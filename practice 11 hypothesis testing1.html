Statistical Analysis Scenarios

Below are ten scenarios requiring a one-sample z-test or t-test. Each scenario provides enough information to perform the test, including sample data. For each, provide the null and alternative hypotheses, choose the testing method (z-test or t-test), and determine whether a one-tail or two-tail test is appropriate. The parameter to be tested is denoted by μ (population mean).

Scenario 1: Factory Machine Output

A factory claims its machines produce widgets with an average weight of 50 grams (μ). The population standard deviation is known to be 2 grams. A sample of 40 widgets has a mean weight of 50.8 grams. Test if the average weight differs from 50 grams. Parameter: μ (population mean weight).

Scenario 2: Student Test Scores

A school claims its students score an average of 75 points (μ) on a standardized test, with population variance unknown. A sample of 25 students has a mean score of 78 points and a sample standard deviation of 5 points. Test if the average score is higher than 75 points. Parameter: μ (population mean score).

Scenario 3: Battery Life

A manufacturer claims their batteries last an average of 100 hours (μ) with a known population standard deviation of 5 hours. A sample of 50 batteries has a mean life of 98 hours. Test if the average battery life is less than 100 hours. Parameter: μ (population mean battery life).

Scenario 4: Coffee Shop Wait Time

A coffee shop claims customers wait an average of 5 minutes (μ) for orders, with population variance unknown. A sample of 30 customers has a mean wait time of 5.4 minutes and a sample standard deviation of 1.2 minutes. Test if the average wait time differs from 5 minutes. Parameter: μ (population mean wait time).

Scenario 5: Drug Effectiveness

A pharmaceutical company claims a drug reduces blood pressure by an average of 10 mmHg (μ) with a known population standard deviation of 1.5 mmHg. A sample of 60 patients has a mean reduction of 10.8 mmHg. Test if the reduction is greater than 10 mmHg. Parameter: μ (population mean reduction).

Scenario 6: Employee Productivity

A company claims employees complete tasks in an average of 40 hours per week (μ), with population variance unknown. A sample of 20 employees has a mean of 42 hours and a sample standard deviation of 3 hours. Test if the average time differs from 40 hours. Parameter: μ (population mean hours).

Scenario 7: Light Bulb Lifespan

A manufacturer claims their light bulbs last an average of 1000 hours (μ) with a known population standard deviation of 50 hours. A sample of 45 bulbs has a mean lifespan of 980 hours. Test if the lifespan is less than 1000 hours. Parameter: μ (population mean lifespan).

Scenario 8: Customer Satisfaction

A store claims an average customer satisfaction score of 4.0 (μ) on a 5-point scale, with population variance unknown. A sample of 35 customers has a mean score of 3.8 and a sample standard deviation of 0.6. Test if the score differs from 4.0. Parameter: μ (population mean satisfaction score).

Scenario 9: Delivery Time

A delivery service claims packages are delivered in an average of 30 minutes (μ) with a known population standard deviation of 4 minutes. A sample of 50 deliveries has a mean time of 31.5 minutes. Test if the average delivery time is greater than 30 minutes. Parameter: μ (population mean delivery time).

Scenario 10: Software Response Time

A software company claims their application has an average response time of 200 milliseconds (μ), with population variance unknown. A sample of 30 tests has a mean response time of 195 milliseconds and a sample standard deviation of 10 milliseconds. Test if the response time is less than 200 milliseconds. Parameter: μ (population mean response time).
